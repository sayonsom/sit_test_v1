# Introduction 

The efficient transmission of electrical power over long distances is a
critical aspect of modern electrical power systems. High voltage
transmission lines are employed to transport electricity from power
generation plants to distribution networks and end-users. However, as
power travels through these transmission lines, various phenomena can
affect the overall performance of the system. One such phenomenon that
demands careful consideration is the Ferranti Effect.

Ferranti Effect refers to the phenomenon where the voltage at the
receiving end of a transmission line is higher than the voltage at the
sending end when the line is lightly loaded or under no load conditions.
This counterintuitive effect can have significant implications for the
design, operation, and protection of power systems. This effect is
primarily attributed to the capacitance of the transmission line, which
causes voltage magnification as the line length increases.

This laboratory experiment aims to investigate the Ferranti Effect on
transmission lines and explore its impact on voltage regulation under
various loading conditions. We will analyse how different parameters,
such as line length, capacitance, and load conditions, influence the
magnitude of the Ferranti Effect. The experimental setup will include a
scaled-down model of a transmission line, allowing for systematic
observations and measurements.

# Theory

The Ferranti Effect on transmission lines is a phenomenon that arises
due to the distributed capacitance of the line. This effect becomes
particularly noticeable when the transmission line is lightly loaded or
under no-load conditions. The theory behind the Ferranti Effect can be
explained through the following key concepts:

## Capacitance of Transmission Lines:

-   Transmission lines inherently possess capacitance between the
    conductors and the surrounding ground. This capacitance allows the
    line to store electrical energy in the form of an electric field
    between the conductors.

-   The capacitance is distributed along the entire length of the
    transmission line and is influenced by factors such as the spacing
    between conductors and the surrounding medium.

## Voltage Distribution and Phase Shift:

-   When electrical power is transmitted over a long-distance
    transmission line, the distributed capacitance causes a phase shift
    between the voltage and current along the line.

-   Capacitive reactance leads to a lagging power factor, causing the
    current to lag behind the voltage in the transmission line.

## Voltage Magnification:

-   Under no-load or light-load conditions, where the current flowing
    through the transmission line is relatively small, the capacitive
    reactance dominates the impedance of the line.

-   The voltage drop across the inductive (resistive) components is
    small compared to the voltage drop across the capacitive reactance.
    As a result, the voltage at the receiving end of the line is higher
    than the voltage at the sending end.

# Objectives

The objectives of the laboratory experiment on the Ferranti Effect on
transmission lines are as follows:

1.  **Investigate Ferranti Effect Magnitude:**

    -   Measure and quantify the Ferranti Effect on a scaled-down
        transmission line under varying load conditions to understand
        how voltage magnification occurs.

2.  **Explore Line Length Influence:**

    -   Examine the impact of varying the length of the transmission
        line on the Ferranti Effect, aiming to determine the
        relationship between line length and voltage amplification.

3.  **Analyse Capacitance Effects:**

    -   Investigate how changes in the capacitance of the transmission
        line affect the Ferranti Effect, providing insights into the
        role of line capacitance in voltage regulation.

4.  **Examine Load Conditions:**

    -   Study the Ferranti Effect under different load scenarios,
        ranging from no-load to full load, to observe how the phenomenon
        responds to varying levels of power demand.

# Procedure

**Baseline Measurements:**

1.  Apply a no-load condition to the transmission line by disconnecting
    the load resistor.

2.  Measure and record the sending end voltage (VS​) and the receiving
    end voltage (VR​) using the calibrated instruments.

3.  Record the current (I) flowing through the transmission line.

4.  Calculate the power factor angle (ϕ) using the measured voltage and
    current.

**Varying Load Conditions:**

1.  Gradually increase the load by adjusting the variable load resistor
    while maintaining constant voltage at the sending end.

2.  At each load increment, record VS​, VR​, and I.

3.  Calculate the power factor angle (ϕ) for each load condition.

**Varying Line Length:**

1.  adjust the length of the transmission line.

2.  Repeat the experiment steps under different line length conditions,
    measuring and recording VS​, VR​, and I.
